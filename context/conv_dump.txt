        The split is clean and plays directly to each person's existing strengths while giving everyone meaningful new things to learn.

The key thing to understand about why this division works: the three roles produce three distinct outputs that feed each other in sequence. The IoT person produces a clean state estimate (via EKF) from noisy sensor data. The AI person takes that clean state as RL observations and trains the policy. The fullstack person takes the trained model and simulation logs and makes them visible and usable. None of them can finish without the others.

A few practical things worth knowing before you start:

The most critical week is week 1. All three of you should sit together and implement the basic Tsiolkovsky rocket equation and a simple 1D trajectory plot — even if it's rough. That shared physics vocabulary is what lets you communicate properly for the remaining 9 weeks. The IoT person needs to know what state variables matter, the fullstack person needs to know what to display, and the AI person needs to know what the physics constraints are.

Your biggest integration risk is the data contract between IoT and AI — specifically what the EKF outputs and what format the RL environment expects as input. Agree on this as a shared Python dataclass or JSON schema in week 2 and don't change it without telling each other. Breaking that interface mid-project is the most common reason student teams stall.

For the fullstack person, Streamlit is faster to prototype than Plotly Dash and perfectly fine for a research demo. Only move to Dash if you need more real-time update control.

For the IoT person, if hardware feels like too much for the timeline, the sensor noise simulation module alone is a solid, publishable contribution — real hardware is a bonus, not a requirement for a strong project.
